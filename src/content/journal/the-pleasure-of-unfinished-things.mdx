There are twelve unfinished paintings in my studio. I know exactly what each one is trying to be, and I know that none of them are there yet. Some have been sitting for over a year.

For a long time this felt like failure.

Now it mostly feels fine. More than fine, actually — there's something I've come to value in the particular quality of unfinished things. They remain possible. They haven't yet committed to being what they are. Every time I look at them, they're still negotiable.

## Finished things close themselves

When a thing is finished, it becomes fixed. You can no longer imagine it differently. The version that exists is the version that will always exist, and all the versions you considered and rejected become hypothetical. This is mostly good — it's how things get made, how they get out into the world. But there's also a small loss in it.

Unfinished things hold their possibilities open. The painting that isn't done yet could still go somewhere unexpected. The software project you set aside mid-refactor contains a decision you haven't had to make yet. There's a spaciousness in that.

## This is not an excuse for not finishing things

I want to be careful here. Some of my unfinished things are genuinely stalled — stuck on a decision I've been avoiding, or waiting for an energy I haven't found. Those aren't pleasurably unfinished. They're just unfinished.

The ones I mean are different. They're resting between stages. They're in the middle of becoming something, and the middle is where they belong right now.

The distinction matters. One is avoidance dressed as patience. The other is patience that actually looks like patience.

---

I finished a painting last week that had been sitting for eight months. It's not the best thing I've ever made. But it was right to wait. I can see, looking at it now, that I needed to not know how to finish it for a while before I could.
