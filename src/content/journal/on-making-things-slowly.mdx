I used to believe that speed was a virtue. Ship fast, iterate fast, fail fast — the whole liturgy. And for a while, it felt right. There was energy in it. The feeling of momentum.

But somewhere along the way I noticed that the things I was most proud of — a painting that took three weekends, a piece of software that I rewrote four times before it felt honest — none of them were fast. They were slow. Uncertain. Full of long pauses where I wasn't sure what to do next.

Slowness, I've come to think, isn't the opposite of good work. It's closer to a precondition for it.

## The problem with always moving

When you're always in motion, you can't tell the difference between progress and activity. Both feel the same from the inside. You're doing things, things are changing — it must be going somewhere. But it isn't always. Sometimes you're just moving to avoid sitting with uncertainty.

Painting taught me this more clearly than any software project could. A canvas will wait. It has no deadline. You can stand in front of it for twenty minutes and do nothing, and it won't judge you for it. Gradually I learned that the standing and looking was part of the work. Maybe the most important part.

## What making slowly actually requires

It requires tolerance for incompleteness. Most of what you make, for most of the time you're making it, looks wrong. Half-finished things are ugly. The gap between what you can see in your head and what exists on the screen or canvas is not comfortable to live in. Slow making means staying in that gap longer.

It requires trust — that something is accumulating even when you can't see it. That the six bad drafts are not wasted, they're load-bearing.

And it requires a certain indifference to other people's timelines. Not contempt — just the ability to say: *this takes as long as it takes.*

---

I'm still learning all three. But I notice I'm less impatient than I used to be. And the things I make feel more like mine.
